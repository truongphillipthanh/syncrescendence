# 5 Ways to Make Money with OpenClaw (Real Examples)
(Description: Cover image with text "5 WAYS TO MAKE MONEY WITH OPENCLAW (REAL EXAMPLES)" alongside an illustration of a red robotic claw holding a money bag, set against a black background)
I didn't install OpenClaw expecting to create a business. It was one of those "let me just try this" moments. You know the type. Peaceful evening. Explore a bit. Maybe get some inspiration. Then back to real work.
(Description: Animated GIF showing a figure running towards a new revenue stream)
But after a few days of actually building with it, something shifted. Not because it seemed advanced. Because it felt useful in a very unsexy way. And useful things tend to make money.
OpenClaw is powerful, sure. But power alone doesn't pay rent. Implementation does. Distribution does. Solving boring, expensive problems does.
I've been testing OpenClaw since day two. Building my own assistants. Watching what other people ship. Paying attention to what actually turns into invoices, not just likes on X.
Here's what I've noticed.
There's a small group of people quietly clearing $10k+ a month with OpenClaw right now. Not because they're geniuses. Not because they discovered some secret API trick. They just picked a model, implemented it cleanly, and sold the outcome.
Here are five of those models.
## 1. Sell "Done-For-You" OpenClaw Setups
Let's start with the most obvious one. Probably the fastest too.
Most business owners want an AI assistant. They just don't want to learn the technology. They don't want to read docs. They don't want to debug integrations. They definitely don't want to spend a weekend figuring out why the calendar sync failed. They want the result. And someone else to deal with the mess.
That's the opportunity.
The basic model: you learn the OpenClaw setup process once. Then you charge businesses to do it for them.
**Typical pricing I'm seeing:**
- Basic assistant (email + calendar + a few workflows): $500–$1,000
- Advanced setup (custom skills, automations, internal tools): $3,000–$5,000+
But here's the part most people miss. The real money isn't in the setup. It's in the retainer.
Once the assistant is live, businesses don't want to maintain it. They want it monitored, improved, updated, and quietly working in the background. So you charge $200–$500/month for maintenance, optimization, and tweaks.
You build it once. They pay you every month.
Think about it. 10 clients on a $300/month retainer = $3,000/month. Add one or two new setups per month and you're already brushing up against five figures.
And you don't need a massive audience to start. Here's how most people get their first clients:
- Build an assistant for yourself
- Document every step (screenshots, notes, Loom videos)
- Show people what it can do
- Someone asks "can you set this up for me?"
That documentation becomes your delivery process. Your playbook. Your leverage. Suddenly you're not "learning OpenClaw." You're running a service business.
## 2. Build and Sell Custom Skills
Skills are where OpenClaw really shines. At a basic level, a skill is a structured set of instructions. You're training the assistant to perform a specific task consistently.
Write proposals. Generate reports. Post content. Manage leads. Handle inventory. Nothing magical. Just repeatable logic.
And yes, you can sell these.
Marketplaces like ClawHub make distribution easy. But the marketplace isn't where the leverage is. The leverage is in niche.
A generic "email assistant" skill? Maybe $20. If you're lucky. But a real estate-specific skill that writes listing descriptions, schedules showings, follows up with leads, and generates CMA summaries? That's a $200 skill without blinking.
Same code. Different context. Completely different value.
Here's the formula that keeps working:
- Pick an industry you already understand
- Identify their repetitive, expensive tasks
- Build skills that replace or accelerate those tasks
- Sell directly to that audience
I've seen people bundle skills together and charge $500–$2,000 for industry-specific packs. And here's the funny part. The narrower the niche, the less price pushback you face.
When someone feels a tool was "built for people like me," price resistance disappears. You're not selling software. You're selling relief.
## 3. 10× Your Freelance Output
This one isn't talked about enough. Maybe because it's not flashy. But it works.
If you're already a freelancer, OpenClaw can quietly multiply your output without multiplying your hours. Writers. Researchers. Virtual assistants. Consultants.
All of these roles have a split between execution and expertise. Execution is the grind: research, first drafts, formatting, client updates, repetitive admin. Expertise is the human part: judgment, taste, strategy, final decisions, relationships.
OpenClaw is very good at execution.
Here's an example. A content writer I know set up an assistant to research topics, generate outlines, draft first versions, and handle basic client communication. Result? They went from 3 clients to 12 clients in the same number of hours. Same workweek. Same energy level. Four times the income.
The trick is being honest about where your value actually comes from. If a task doesn't require your judgment, taste, or experience — it probably shouldn't require your time.
Set it up once. Document the process. Let the assistant handle the repetitive stuff. Your effective hourly rate climbs without you working longer days.
Honestly, that's the dream.
## 4. AI Assistant as a Service
This is where things get interesting.
I've talked to multiple business owners recently who all said the same thing, just in different words: "I don't want to use an AI assistant. I want someone to run it for me."
They don't want a setup and a handoff. They want an operator. Someone who manages the assistant. Monitors performance. Tweaks workflows. Improves outputs over time. They want results, not dashboards.
This leads to a simple but powerful model: AI assistant as a service.
**Typical pricing:** $500–$2,000/month per client, depending on complexity and usage.
**Your costs?** Mostly API fees — usually $50–$200/month per client. Margins are strong. And once your workflows are dialed in, you can manage multiple clients with similar setups.
Realistically, one person can manage 10–20 clients on largely standardized systems with documented processes. Do the math. Even on the low end, that's serious recurring revenue.
The ideal clients here are busy founders. Agencies. Professional service firms. Anyone who values time more than money.
Start with one client. Prove it works. Turn it into a case study. Then scale carefully. This isn't a hustle. It's an operations business.
## 5. Courses, Templates, and Practical Education
The education market around AI assistants is kind of a mess right now. Most content falls into one of two camps: way too technical, or way too shallow.
Business owners don't want theory. They want implementation. They want to know what to build. How to set it up. Where people get stuck. How to avoid wasting time.
That's why practical education sells.
**What's working right now:**
- Setup guides for specific use cases: $47–$197
- Skill bundles with walkthroughs: $97–$497
- Full courses on AI-powered businesses: $497–$2,000+
- Template libraries (prompts, skills, workflows): $27–$97
And no, you don't have to be the top expert in the world. You only need to stay a step ahead of your audience and articulate what you've learned clearly.
The play is simple:
- Document what you build
- Turn real implementations into templates
- Package them with clear instructions
- Sell to people slightly earlier in the journey
If you're already building anyway, this is just leverage.
## Where Do You Start?
Not with all five. Pick one.
Honestly, the fastest path to revenue for most people is still this: Build something useful for yourself. Learn the setup process properly. Then offer to set it up for someone else.
Your first client usually comes from showing, not selling.
Zooming out for a second. The AI assistant space right now looks like social media marketing in 2015. Early enough for positioning to matter. Developed enough that people are already spending.
The people learning this now will have a massive head start. Not because the skill is hard. But because they started before it was obvious.
