# The Universal Hierarchy of Life

## Executive Summary
Chris Kempes, professor at Santa Fe Institute, presents a universal framework for understanding life across substrates. He proposes a three-level hierarchy: materials (infinitely diverse), constraints (physical laws all must obey), and principles (optimization/computation that define "alive"). This framework suggests consciousness, culture, and AI could all be forms of life when evaluated at the principles level. Assembly theory offers a mathematical method to identify biotic vs abiotic complexity through recursive part-reuse patterns.

## Key Insights

### Three-Level Hierarchy of Life
Life can be understood through three levels: materials (maximum diversity—alien life could use completely different chemistry), constraints (physics everyone must obey—thermodynamics, diffusion), and principles (abstract optimization processes like evolution, learning, computation). The higher the level, the more universal and convergent.

### Convergence Across Substrates
Evolution repeatedly finds similar solutions to similar problems because physics constrains what works. Eyes evolved independently multiple times. Flight evolved in insects, birds, bats, pterosaurs. This convergence happens not just biologically but algorithmically—problem-solving strategies that work in evolution also work in culture and AI.

### Culture and AI as Life
In a functionalist view, if we define life by abstract principles—evolution, learning, computation, information processing—then human culture is a living system. So is AI. Memes are analogous to genes: they vary, are selected, and the ones better at spreading become more common.

### Phase Transitions in Evolution
Evolution exhibits phase changes—sudden qualitative shifts in organization. Scaling relationships predict hard limits for each organizational level. At these "walls," new architectures must be invented. The transition from bacteria to eukaryotes, from unicellular to multicellular—each required fundamentally different organization.

### Assembly Theory as Biosignature
Assembly theory (developed with Lee Cronin and Sara Walker) quantifies complexity through shortest-path recursive construction. It asks: how many unique steps to build this object from parts you can reuse? This provides a substrate-independent measure of whether something was made by a living process. Experimentally, there appears to be a sharp threshold between abiotic and biotic assembly complexity.

### David Deutsch's Matter/Logic Separation
Understanding certain architectures requires recognizing multiple levels of description. A computer's physical description (electrons moving) leaves out crucial information—you need the logical/software notion to understand what it's doing. This separation is key to understanding life.
