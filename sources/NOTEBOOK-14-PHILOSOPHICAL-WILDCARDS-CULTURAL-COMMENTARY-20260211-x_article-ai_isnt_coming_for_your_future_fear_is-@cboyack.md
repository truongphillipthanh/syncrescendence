## I â€“ The seen and the unseen

> "There is only one difference between a bad economist and a good one: the bad economist confines himself to the **visible** effect; the good economist takes into account both the effect that can be **seen** and those effects that must be **foreseen**." â€”FrÃ©dÃ©ric Bastiat, 1850

This single idea, written over 175 years ago, is the master key to understanding every AI doomer headline you've ever read.

Call it **the seen and the unseen**. It's devastatingly simple.

When a new technology arrives, certain effects are immediately visible. You can **see** the assembly-line worker whose job has just been automated. You can **see** the copywriter watching Grok produce in seconds what used to take her hours. You can **see** the customer service team being replaced by a chatbot built with Claude Code in a matter of minutes.

This is "the seen." It's tangible. It's emotional. It has a human face. And it makes for incredible content, because fear and loss are among the most powerful drivers of engagement. (This is exactly why those doom articles get millions of impressions. Fear is viral. Opportunity is not. Hence, this article will get far fewer sharesâ€¦ help fix that by reposting it? ðŸ™)

But there is a second category of effectsâ€¦ the ones Bastiat said "emerge only subsequently." These are **the unseen**. The new industries that don't exist yet. The businesses that become possible only **because** costs have dropped. The creative work that gets unlocked when drudgery disappears. The entrepreneur who can now build alone what used to require a team of twenty. The consumer who now has access to something that was previously unaffordable.

The unseen is, by definition, harder to see.

That's the whole point.

And it's why the bad economist (or the bad forecaster, or the panicked scrolling reader) always gets it wrong. They stare at the seen, extrapolate doom, and completely miss the explosion of new opportunity forming just outside their field of vision.

I consider this **the** critical thinking error of our time. And it's not limited to economists or pundits. It affects how **you** are probably thinking about your own career right now.

When you read a headline that says "AI will replace 40% of jobs," your brain does something very specific: it imagines 40% of **current** workers losing their **current** jobs and sitting idle. It does not (it **cannot**) simultaneously imagine the new roles, new industries, and new forms of value that will be created. Because those things don't exist yet. They're unseen.

I don't think this is a failure of intelligence per se. It's more a failure of imagination that is baked into how human cognition works.

But history can show us what our imagination can't. And history's record on this is unambiguous.

## II â€“ A queen, a clergyman, and a knitting machine

In 1589, an English clergyman named William Lee invented something remarkable: a mechanical knitting frame that could produce stockings far faster than any human hand.

He brought it to Queen Elizabeth I, hoping for a royal patent. She refused.

In a story recorded centuries later, Elizabeth I is said to have told William Lee, "Thou aimest high, Master Leeâ€¦ It would assuredly bring, to [hand knitters], ruin by depriving them of employment, thus making them beggars."

Read that again.

A monarch (reportedly) looked at a machine that would make clothing faster, cheaper, and more accessible to her subjects, and her instinct was to **block it**. Not because it didn't work but because it worked too well. Because she could see the hand knitters who would lose their immediate livelihood, and she could not see everything that would come after.

Lee was forced to flee to France, where he died in poverty.

But the technology didn't die with him. His brother brought the knitting frames back to England. The machines spread. And over the next two centuries, that very same textile industryâ€”the one Elizabeth tried to protect by freezing it in placeâ€”became the engine of the Industrial Revolution.

England didn't just keep its textile jobs. It **became the wealthiest nation on the planet** **because** of mechanized textile production. The industry grew orders of magnitude beyond what hand knitters could ever have produced. New jobs, new trades, and new entire economic classes emerged that Elizabeth couldn't have fathomed.

She saw the hand knitters. She could not see the factory workers, the merchants, the engineers, the shippers, the global trade networks, the rising middle classâ€¦ and the entire modern economy that the knitting machine helped set in motion.

"The seen" destroyed her judgment. "The unseen" would have saved it.

## III â€“ The Luddites smashed the future

Fast forward about 220 years from Lee's invention.

It's 1811, and in England (in the same textile industry), new machines like power looms and shearing frames are arriving in factories and producing cloth faster and cheaper than skilled artisans can by hand.

The craftsmen are terrified. They had spent years in apprenticeships. They had families to provide for. And now a machine operated by a child could produce in a day what took them a week.

So they organized and called themselves the Luddites (after a perhaps-mythical apprentice named Ned Ludd who had smashed a stocking frame in a fit of rage). They launched a campaign of nighttime raids, breaking into factories, destroying hundreds of machines with sledgehammers, and burning mills to the ground.

Parliament made machine-breaking a capital offense. At one point, the British government deployed thousands of soldiers to fight the Luddites.

The Luddites were crushed and the machines stayed. So what happened next?

The British textile industry didn't shrink. It **exploded**. By the end of the century, it employed **far** more people than it had before mechanization. The cloth was cheaper, so demand soared. Exports created entirely new markets. Supporting industries like shipping, coal, iron, engineering, and finance emerged to serve the growing machine. The Luddites could see their own displacement. They couldn't see the vast expansion of opportunity that was forming just behind it.

And now their name is a permanent labelâ€¦ a synonym for someone who fights the future out of fear.

It's worth asking: do you want to be remembered the way they are?

## IV â€“ This pattern never stops

Don't write these stories off as occasional anecdotes from history. It's not that this happened once or twice. It happens **every single time**.

In the 1920s, a book called *Social Decay and Regeneration* was reviewed by the *New York Times* under the headline "Will Machines Devour Man?" â€” illustrated with a drawing of a person being fed into a sausage-making machine. The president of the Rockefeller Foundation asked at a commencement speech whether education could "run fast enough" to beat the machines. What followed that decade of fear? In the 1960s, fears of automation were so intense that President Johnson formed a "National Commission on Technology, Automation, and Economic Progress" to study whether machines would create permanent mass unemployment. Eisenhower dismissed the panic, noting that the same fears had "plagued people for 150 years and always proved groundless." What followed? The postwar boom, the space age, the computing revolution, decades of rising wages, and entirely new industries no one had imagined.

When ATMs arrived in the 1970s, everyone was sure bank tellers were finished. A 1973 article predicted they'd eliminate up to 75% of tellers. London bank tellers were reportedly so panicked they snuck out and covered ATM keyboards with honey. What actually happened? Between 1985 and 2002, the US went from 60,000 ATMs to 352,000â€¦ and teller employment **grew** from 485,000 to 527,000. ATMs made branches cheaper to run, so banks opened more branches, and shifted tellers into higher-value relationship roles. The technology created **more** human jobs.

When barcode scanners hit retail, people predicted the end of cashiers. The number of cashiers increased. When legal offices adopted electronic discovery software, people predicted the end of paralegals. The number of paralegals increased. When spreadsheet software arrived, people predicted the end of accountants. The accounting profession grew.

Every single time, the pattern is the same: people see what's being disrupted, project catastrophe, and completely fail to foresee the new opportunities, industries, and human flourishing that the technology unlocks.

## V â€“ The fixed-pie delusion

So why do smart people keep getting this wrong?

Because of **the fixed-pie delusion**â€”the deeply ingrained, almost instinctual belief that the economy is a zero-sum game. That there is a fixed amount of work to be done, and if a machine does more of it, humans must do less.

This feels intuitively true, I guess. It's also completely, demonstrably false.

The economy is not a pie. It's a garden. And technology is rain.

When the power loom arrived, the "pie" wasn't cloth production. The pie expanded to include global textile trade, chemical dyes, synthetic fabrics, fashion as an industry, retail distribution networks, and eventually fast fashion, e-commerce, and everything that flows from people having access to affordable clothing.

When the automobile arrived, the "pie" wasn't horse transportation. It expanded to include suburbs, highway systems, motels, fast food, drive-in theaters, road trips as a cultural phenomenon, auto insurance, gas stations, auto repair, car washes, and an entire civilization organized around personal mobility.

Each technology didn't just redistribute existing value. It **created** value that didn't exist before. It expanded what was possible. And in that expansion, new workâ€”often better, more interesting, more human workâ€”emerged.

> "It is much easier to imagine someone losing their job to a new technology, than it is to imagine many people gaining jobs that haven't been invented yet."
> â€” Pessimists Archive

This is Bastiat's insight in its most potent form. The seen (job losses) are vivid and personal. The unseen (job creation in industries that don't yet exist) is abstract and therefore invisible.

But invisibility is not the same as nonexistence.

## VII â€“ "But this time is differentâ€¦"

Let's anticipate and rebut the most likely objection.

"Sure, the Luddites were wrong. Sure, ATMs didn't kill bank tellers. But AI is **different**. It's not just automating physical laborâ€¦ it's automating **thought**. It's coming for cognitive work, creative work, the things that were supposed to be uniquely human. This time really is different!"

Let me take that seriously, because it deserves a serious answer. It's not a dumb objection. It's the strongest version of the pessimist's case, and if I'm going to ask you to reject it, I should show you exactly why.

Yes, AI is different in its **speed**. Previous technological shifts played out over decades. AI is moving in months. That compression is real and it matters. The adjustment period will be shorter, which means the disruption will feel more intense.

Yes, AI is different in its **breadth**. It touches knowledge work, creative work, and service work simultaneously. A power loom only disrupted one industry. AI is touching all of them at once. That's unprecedented.

But here's what AI is **not** different in:

**it is still a tool.**

A power loom was a tool. An automobile was a tool. A computer was a tool. The internet was a tool. Each one automated tasks that people previously did by hand (or by brain). Each one was met with existential panic. But each one expanded the pie.

The fundamental economic dynamic hasn't changed: when a tool makes something cheaper or faster, demand increases, new applications emerge, and human effort redirects toward higher-order problems that the tool **can't** solve alone.

Think about what happened with the internet. In 1995, people were convinced it would destroy retail, publishing, travel agencies, music, and dozens of other industries. And in a narrow sense, they were rightâ€¦ **specific forms** of those industries were disrupted. Some local bookstores did close. The travel agent did lose her job. Video killed the radio star.

But the internet didn't shrink the economy. It created a multi-trillion-dollar digital economy, launched millions of businesses, and invented entire job categories like social media manager, UX designer, data scientist, content creator, SEO specialist, cloud architect, app developer, influencer, podcast producer, e-commerce entrepreneurâ€¦ these and many more jobs literally did not exist before.

The pessimists in 1995 could see the bookstore closing. They couldn't see Shopify, YouTube, Stripe, the entire creator economy, remote work as a lifestyle, or the fact that a teenager with a laptop could build a global business from a bedroom.

That's what it looks like when you can only see the seen.

The "this time is different" argument has also been made **every single previous time**. The 1960s automation panic specifically centered on the claim that **this** wave of automation was different from previous ones because machines were now doing **mental** work (early computing), not just physical work. That was the exact same argument being made today, 60 years ago. It was wrong then, too.

Will AI be faster? Yes. Will the transition be bumpy? Absolutely. Will some specific jobs disappear permanently? Of course.

But will AI shrink the total amount of meaningful human work? Will it lead to mass permanent unemployment?

Every piece of evidence from every technological shift in the last 500 years says no. And the burden of proof is on the pessimists to explain why **this** timeâ€”the pattern that has held for five centuries will suddenly reverse.

## VIII â€“ How to position yourself for what's coming

So if fear is the wrong response, what's the right one? Here's what I'd suggest.

### 1) Use the tools. Today. Not tomorrow.

This is the single most important thing you can do. Don't read another article about AI. Open Grok, Claude, or ChatGPT, and give it a real task from your actual work. Something you'd normally spend an hour on. See what happens.

The gap between people who **talk about AI** and people who **use AI daily** is enormous. Cross that gap.

### 2) Think "with," not "instead of."

The frame that will serve you best is not "will AI replace me?" but "what can I do **with** AI that I couldn't do before?"

This is the difference between a bank teller in 1980 thinking "the ATM is taking my job" and thinking "the ATM is handling the boring stuff so I can actually help customers with complex problems." One framing leads to panic. The other leads to a promotion.

### 3) Expand your ambition.

Here's something subtle that nobody's talking about: AI should make you **more** ambitious, not less.

If it now takes you 2 hours to do what used to take 20, the correct response isn't to work 2 hours and call it a day. It's to ask: what was I unable to attempt before that I can attempt now? What project was too expensive? What idea was too complex? What market was too hard to reach?

The people who will do extraordinarily well in the AI era are the ones who use the leverage to aim **higher**, not the ones who use it to coast.

### 4) Invest in what AI can't do.

AI is phenomenal at generating, summarizing, analyzing, and pattern-matching. It is not good (yet) at: original judgment, deep relationship building, physical-world problem solving, moral reasoning, cultural intuition, or leading people through ambiguity.

Double down on those skills. They become **more** valuable as AI handles everything else.

### 5) Study history â€” and refuse to repeat it.

Read the Pessimists Archive. Study the Luddites. Read Bastiat. Understand that the fear you're feeling has been felt by millions of people before you, across centuries, and that **it has always been wrong at scale**.

This doesn't mean change won't be painful for some people in the short term. It will! Transitions are real. But the macro trajectory of every major technological shift in human history has been: more prosperity, more opportunity, more human flourishing. Not less.

Bet on that pattern. It has a centuries-long track record.
