### Foundation Roadmap

**2/29** The roadmap has two parts. The first 25 threads follow this diagram: Algebra → Geometry → Trigonometry → Precalculus → Calculus → Linear Algebra & Multivariable Calculus. After that, we map this math to real careers. Make sure to bookmark, you'll need it later.

**3/29** 1. Start with algebra. Learn to manipulate symbols cleanly. If you struggle here, everything later becomes noise. 2. Algebra is about rules and structure, not calculation speed. Slow down until each step feels obvious.

**4/29** 3. Learn equations as cause–effect relations. Don't solve, understand what changes what. 4. Geometry comes next. Learn how shapes, distances, and angles behave. This trains intuition that formulas alone won't give.

**5/29** 5. Treat geometry visually and algebraically at the same time. Translate pictures into equations and back. 6. Introduce vectors early. They unify algebra and geometry. Don't delay this step.

**6/29** 7. Trigonometry is about angles and rotation, not triangles. Focus on sine and cosine as functions. 8. Learn trig through the unit circle, not identities. If you can draw it, you understand it.

**7/29** 9. Precalculus connects functions, trig, and algebra. Its job is to make calculus predictable. 10. If precalc feels unclear, stop and fix it. Calculus punishes weak foundations.

**8/29** 11. Calculus starts with derivatives as rates of change. Forget formulas until the idea is clear. 12. A derivative answers: "What happens if I change this slightly"? Practice asking that question constantly.

**9/29** 13. Use calculus to model behavior, not to compute expressions. Most real problems stop at setup. 14. Integration is accumulation. Area is just one example. Always ask: "What is being added up?"

**10/29** 15. Calculus II is hard because it's technical. Don't memorize tricks, learn when each tool applies. 16. Learn limits conceptually, not mechanically. They explain why calculus works at all.

**11/29** 17. Study logic and basic proofs next. This sharpens reasoning and prevents hidden assumptions. 18. Proofs train discipline, not elegance. Follow arguments line by line until nothing feels magical.

**12/29** 19. Linear algebra should come early, not late. It explains structure in many-variable systems. 20. Think of matrices as transformations, not tables. This mental shift matters.

**13/29** 21. Eigenvalues describe stability and long-term behavior. They're ideas, not calculations. 22. Multivariable calculus reflects reality. Most systems depend on more than one input.

**14/29** 23. Partial derivatives isolate influence. Gradients show direction, not answers. 24. Learn constrained problems explicitly. Unconstrained math is rarely real. 25. Only after this, study probability and statistics. Uncertainty makes sense once change and structure do.

### Career Applications

**15/29** Foundation done. Applications start here: 26. Engineering: Calculus + linear algebra + constraints. Used for modeling, optimization, and stability. 27. Machine Learning: Linear algebra + probability + optimization. Everything else is implementation detail.

**16/29** 28. Data Science: Statistics + linear algebra + basic calculus. Focus on assumptions, not tools. 29. Finance: Calculus + probability + linear systems. Rates of change and risk dominate.
