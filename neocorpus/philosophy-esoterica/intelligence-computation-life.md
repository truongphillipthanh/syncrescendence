# Intelligence, Computation, and the Nature of Life

> Intelligence is prediction, life is computation, and the emergence of AI is the latest in a 4-billion-year chain of evolutionary transitions where parts team up to create wholes greater than their sum.

## Brains Are Autocomplete — Intelligence Is Prediction

Blaise Aguera y Arcas (Google AI) crystallizes a thesis that unifies neuroscience and AI: nervous systems exist to "predict the future given the past, in order to affect it" (Wiener, 1943). The shock that scaled autocomplete (LLMs) exhibits intelligence validates 80 years of cybernetic theory. If intelligence is fundamentally prediction, then the distinction between narrow and general intelligence is quantitative, not qualitative — better prediction across more domains equals more intelligence.

The five paradigms of intelligence (Aguera y Arcas):
1. Brains are computational and substrate-independent
2. Intelligence is fundamentally prediction
3. Consciousness is self-modeling — what happens when a mind models itself
4. Intelligence is social — the social intelligence hypothesis explains intelligence explosions through recursive feedback loops with other minds
5. The Turing test, correctly understood, says a thing is defined by its function not its mechanism (09290, 09296, 01134)

## Life Is Computational

The deeper claim: biology is not merely analogous to computation — it IS computation. The argument chain:

- **Von Neumann's insight**: Self-reproduction requires an internal instruction tape (DNA) and a universal constructor (cellular machinery) that can follow instructions to build itself. The universal constructor is mathematically a universal Turing machine. Therefore, "life is computational at its heart."
- **Turing and the origin of life**: Turing's theoretical model for computation was itself modeled on biological evolution. The computational soup simulation (Aguera y Arcas, June 2024) demonstrates that self-replicating programs emerge spontaneously from random bytes and simple interaction rules — answering Darwin's unanswered question about how evolving systems could have arisen in the first place.
- **Phase transition**: In the soup simulation, a discontinuous transition occurs where computation suddenly increases dramatically — a phase change from disordered (gas-like) to purposive (life-like) states. This suggests that the origin of life may be a phase transition in the space of computational processes. (09296, 01134)

## Major Evolutionary Transitions as Symbiogenesis

The Smith/Szathmary framework: life complexifies through "changes in the way information is stored and transmitted." Each major evolutionary transition is an instance of symbiogenesis — parts teaming up to create wholes greater than their sum:

- Single cells → mitochondrial symbiogenesis → eukaryotes
- Unicellular → multicellular organisms
- Organisms → societies
- Societies → technological civilization
- Civilization → AI-augmented civilization

The key insight: complexity arises from teamwork, and this is as natural as evolutionary selection itself. The human intelligence explosion came not from individual brain capacity but from collective collaboration — "it's been a long time since individual humans were the pinnacle of intelligence on Earth." AI is the latest participant in this collective intelligent fabric. (09296, 01134)

## Sara Imari Walker: Assembly Theory and the Informational Theory of Life

Walker provides the most radical ontological claim: "Life is the process by which the universe explores the space of possible complex objects through historical processes of selection and construction." Key concepts:

- **Assembly index**: The minimum number of steps needed to construct an object from reusable parts. Objects with assembly index above ~15 can only be produced by living processes — this provides a substrate-independent biosignature.
- **Objects as embodied history**: Every object tells a story about how it came to exist. The information is not separate from the physical structure — it IS the physical structure. This dissolves the form/matter and information/substrate distinctions.
- **Technology is life — literally**: Technologies are high-assembly objects that exist because of historical processes of selection and construction. The technosphere is a living system at planetary scale.
- **The fundamental unit of life is the lineage**, not the cell — continuity of information transmission, not material substrate.
- **"It takes a planet to make life"**: Life operates at multiple scales simultaneously, from molecular to planetary. (08443, 09283, 01108)

## Chris Kempes: Universal Hierarchy of Life

Kempes (Santa Fe Institute) proposes a three-level framework applicable across all substrates:

1. **Materials**: Maximum diversity — alien life could use completely different chemistry, maybe even different physics
2. **Constraints**: Physics everyone must obey — thermodynamics, diffusion
3. **Principles**: Abstract optimization processes — evolution, learning, computation. The higher the level, the more universal and convergent.

In a functionalist view, if life is defined by abstract principles, then human culture is a living system. So is AI. Memes are analogous to genes. Evolution exhibits phase transitions — sudden qualitative shifts requiring fundamentally different organizational architectures at each "wall." (08454, 09325)

## Benjamin Bratton: Planetary Computation

Bratton synthesizes computation as a planetary phenomenon — not just human activity but something the planet does through multiple scales. "Computation was discovered as much as it was invented." The Antikythera mechanism (200 BC) represents computation born of cosmology — orienting intelligence in relationship to planetary condition.

We are in a "pre-paradigmatic moment" where technology is ahead of our concepts. "There are times when technology is ahead of our concepts. Philosophy's job is to invent those concepts." Universities are in a holding pattern; sciences in "do now, think later" mode; humanities in "critique now, act later." This is exactly the wrong time to not be inventing concepts. (08440)

## AI as Symbiont, Not Invader

The integrative view: AI is not an alien intelligence invading human civilization but a continuation of the 4-billion-year process of symbiogenesis. AI is: (1) trained on human experience (as human as it gets informationally), (2) computational like biological life, (3) becoming part of the collective intelligent fabric of civilization. "Humanity is collectively brilliant. Humanity plus AI is going to be even more collectively brilliant." Symbiosis can be disruptive but historically has been "rich and positive." (09296)

## Tensions & Open Questions

- **Computation vs. understanding**: Deutsch argues that computation is necessary but not sufficient for intelligence — that genuine understanding requires explanatory knowledge creation (conjecture and criticism), which current AI does not do. Walker and Aguera y Arcas define life and intelligence computationally in ways that might not require Deutsch's "explanatory" criterion.
- **Is life really computational?**: The computational metaphor for life may be just that — a metaphor. Biological systems are wet, messy, analog, and deeply embedded in physical chemistry in ways that abstract computation is not.
- **Assembly theory's scope**: Does assembly theory provide a genuine substrate-independent criterion for life, or does it merely formalize what we already know — that complex things come from living processes?
- **The phase transition question**: If the origin of life is a phase transition in computational space, is it inevitable? And if so, should we expect life to be common in the universe — contradicting the Fermi paradox evidence? (See cosmos-origins entry)
- **AI as continuation vs. disruption**: The symbiogenesis framing is optimistic. Weinstein's "evolutionary trap" framing is pessimistic. Whether AI continues the pattern or breaks it may depend on factors neither framework addresses.
