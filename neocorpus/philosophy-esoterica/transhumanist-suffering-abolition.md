## David Pearce and the Abolitionist Project

David Pearce, philosopher and bioethicist, is the foremost advocate of what he calls the "abolitionist project" — the systematic elimination of suffering through biotechnology. His central thesis: transhumanism is not merely about enhancement but about the moral imperative to end all suffering. The Foresight Institute, a nonprofit supporting beneficial development of high-impact technologies, provides the institutional home for this discourse.

Pearce's vision encompasses four pillars: (1) overcoming aging, (2) achieving radical life extension, (3) embracing cognitive enhancement, and (4) ending all suffering. These are not separable goals — they constitute a unified program for transcending biological limitations. The suffering abolition component is the most philosophically radical: it claims that the hedonic treadmill, the tendency of organisms to return to baseline well-being regardless of circumstance, can be recalibrated at the genetic and neurochemical level.

## The Philosophical Architecture

The argument rests on several premises:
- Suffering serves evolutionary fitness functions (threat avoidance, social bonding through shared pain) that are no longer adaptive in technologically advanced civilizations
- The neurochemical substrates of suffering (cortisol cascades, pain receptor activation, depressive neural patterns) are biochemically tractable
- Pearce argues that a "paradise engineering" approach can redesign the hedonic set-point upward without eliminating the informational content that negative states currently carry
- The framework proposes that there is no metaphysical necessity to suffering — that it is a contingent feature of biological evolution, not an intrinsic property of consciousness

## Tensions & Open Questions

- **The information problem**: Does suffering carry irreplaceable information? If negative affect signals danger, can the signal be preserved without the phenomenological experience of pain?
- **Identity and meaning**: Many philosophical and religious traditions (Buddhism, Stoicism, Christianity) treat suffering as constitutive of meaning, growth, or spiritual development. Would abolishing suffering abolish a dimension of human experience that generates wisdom?
- **Distribution and access**: Biotechnological interventions for hedonic recalibration would initially be available only to the wealthy, potentially creating unprecedented inequality in the most fundamental dimension of experience.
- **The naturalistic objection**: Critics argue that "paradise engineering" is hubris — that redesigning the emotional architecture of consciousness courts catastrophic unintended consequences.
- **Substrate independence**: If consciousness is substrate-independent (as many in the AI consciousness debate argue), then suffering might be a property of certain computational patterns, not just biological chemistry — making the abolition project harder but also more universal in scope.

## Obsolescence and Supersession

### Suffering as Fixed Hardware — Under Revision
The pre-transhumanist consensus treated suffering as biologically immutable: an intrinsic feature of nervous systems that could be managed pharmacologically but not fundamentally redesigned. Pearce's abolitionist project challenges this directly, proposing that the hedonic set-point is a programmable parameter, not a fixed constant.
