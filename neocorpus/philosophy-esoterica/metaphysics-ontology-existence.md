# Metaphysics, Ontology, and the Nature of Existence

> What exists? How do we know? And what are the fundamental structures of reality? The perennial questions of metaphysics are being transformed by computational ontology, mathematical philosophy, and constructor theory.

## Does Anything Exist? — Ontological Foundations

Alex O'Connor and Michael Stevens (Vsauce) probe the most fundamental ontological question. Key positions:
- **Mereological nihilism**: Only fundamental particles exist; tables, chairs, and people are convenient fictions. Stevens explores whether he is a mereological nihilist — whether composite objects genuinely exist or are just arrangements of simples.
- **The function problem**: Are philosophers overcomplicating functional behavior? Ordinary objects are defined by their functions, not their metaphysical status.
- **Philip Goff's contribution**: "Galileo's Error" argues that science's quantitative method systematically excludes consciousness — it was designed to, as Galileo deliberately expelled qualities from the physical world. (09553, 01614)

## Van Fraassen and Constructive Empiricism

Bas van Fraassen's "radical claim about what you are" extends constructive empiricism to the self. Science aims at empirical adequacy, not truth — our best theories are tools for saving the phenomena, not mirrors of reality. Applied to personal identity, this suggests that the self is a theoretical construct useful for organizing experience but not a metaphysical entity. (09788)

## Wolfram's Computational Ontology

Stephen Wolfram proposes the Ruliad — the entangled limit of all possible computations — as the fundamental structure underlying physics, mathematics, and biology. Key claims:

- **Ruliad + observer = physics**: The Ruliad is every possible computation. Physics arises from how particular observers sample this infinite structure. Different observers (including potential aliens) might sample the Ruliad differently, experiencing entirely different physical laws.
- **Observers create physics**: Not in a subjective idealist sense, but in the sense that the regularities we call "physical law" are artifacts of the particular way our cognitive architecture samples the Ruliad.
- **No AI has impressed Wolfram**: Despite (or because of) his computational framework, Wolfram is unimpressed by current AI — it emulates patterns without understanding the computational structures that generate them. (09724, 09792)

## David Deutsch: Constructor Theory and Knowledge Creation

Deutsch offers the most rigorous philosophical framework for distinguishing AI from AGI:

- **AGI is not AI scaled up**: The difference is qualitative, not quantitative. Current AI performs pattern matching and prediction; AGI would require explanatory knowledge creation — generating new explanations through conjecture and criticism (Popperian epistemology).
- **Constructor theory**: Physics should be formulated in terms of what transformations are possible and impossible, not in terms of dynamical laws. This provides deeper foundations and connects computation theory to fundamental physics.
- **LLMs are universal mimics, not universal explainers**: They can reproduce patterns found in training data but cannot generate genuinely novel explanations that go beyond available data.
- **Quantum may be necessary for consciousness**: If creativity involves quantum effects, classical computers categorically cannot replicate it. (08447, 09301, 01140)

## Mathematics, Infinity, and Godel

Joel David Hamkins (the #1 highest-rated user on MathOverflow) explores infinity, paradoxes, Godel's incompleteness theorems, and the mathematical multiverse. The philosophical implications of Godel: any sufficiently powerful formal system is either incomplete or inconsistent. This has been variously interpreted as showing (a) the limits of mechanistic thinking, (b) the necessity of mathematical intuition beyond formal proof, or (c) the existence of mathematical truth independent of human minds.

Edward Frenkel extends this through the Langlands programme — mathematics' "theory of everything" — and argues for the enduring power of human intuition and creativity in the age of AI. Self-knowledge, he suggests, is key to avoiding malevolent AI — not technical alignment but deeper understanding of what we are. (09862, 09917)

## Free Will and Process Philosophy

Matt Segall ("Free Will Exists But Not In Your Body") articulates a process philosophy position: free will is real but does not reside in the physical body as traditionally conceived. Rather, it is a property of the process of becoming — the creative advance of reality. This draws on Whitehead's process philosophy where experience, not matter, is ontologically fundamental. Each moment of experience involves a "decision" — a cutting-off of alternatives — that constitutes genuine freedom. (09924)

## Quantum Mechanics and Philosophy

Matthew Kinsella (CEO of quantum technology company Infleqtion) provides an unusual intersection: a technical CEO who reflects on quantum mechanics as occupying "the conceptual space religion traditionally held — the boundary between the known and unknown." His key insight: binary logic (zeros and ones) is a heuristic for simulating reality, while quantum computers are reality at the quantum level. "Nature behaves by the laws of quantum mechanics," and forcing quantum phenomena into classical binary representations loses essential information.

John Martinis' Nobel-winning work on macroscopic quantum tunneling proved that billions of electrons can act coherently as a single quantum particle — demonstrating that quantum effects are not confined to the microscale. (08450, 09317, 01167, 09338)

## Meta-Narrative and Perspectival Schemas

A comprehensive framework (04470, 94 atoms) proposes multi-scale analysis of transformation: macro (planetary/civilizational), meso (systemic/institutional), and micro (phenomenological/experiential). Key insights:
- Transformations are nested, not parallel: the physical substrate creates context, systemic architectures mediate it, and the interior landscape experiences life within those systems.
- Most critical insights emerge from understanding scale interactions — macro constraints creating meso crises that manifest as micro trauma.
- The aesthetic dimension (beauty, craft, sensory experience) is essential to human flourishing, not a luxury. (04470)

## Assembly Theory and Ontology

Lee Cronin's assembly theory (via 02382) proposes that selection and evolution can occur even before biological life — that the universe is doing selection at the chemical level. The assembly index (minimum steps to construct an object) provides a substrate-independent measure of whether something was made by a living process. The connection between gravity and the emergence of life is proposed. (02382)

## Tensions & Open Questions

- **Computational ontology vs. mathematical Platonism**: Is reality fundamentally computational (Wolfram) or is mathematics an independent realm that computation merely accesses (Frenkel, Hamkins)?
- **The observer problem**: Both Wolfram and Hoffman place the observer at the center of physics. But who or what counts as an observer? Does observation require consciousness, or is any physical interaction sufficient?
- **Deutsch vs. the scaling hypothesis**: Deutsch insists that AGI requires qualitatively different architecture (explanatory knowledge creation). The scaling hypothesis says more compute and data will eventually produce genuine understanding. The question is empirically open.
- **Free will and determinism**: Segall's process philosophy, Faggin's quantum free will, and the Stoics' rational determinism represent three incompatible frameworks for the same question.
- **The limits of formalism**: Godel shows that formal systems have inherent limits. Does this limit apply to consciousness, computation, and reality itself — or only to our representations of them?
