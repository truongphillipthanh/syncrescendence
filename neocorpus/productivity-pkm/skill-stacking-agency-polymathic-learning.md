## Core Thesis
The most important skill for the next decade is not any particular technical competency but agency — the capacity to set direction, acquire whatever skills are needed, and adapt when conditions change (00019). Agency is the meta-skill that makes all other skills acquirable. In an AI world, this matters more because AI acts as a forcing function on agency (02931): it commoditizes execution skills while amplifying the gap between directed and undirected people. The practical expression of agency is skill stacking — becoming rare not through single-skill mastery but through the unique intersection of multiple top-25% competencies.

## Key Frameworks

### 1. Agency as the Master Skill (00019, 02931)
Agency is defined as the ability to set your own life direction, execute the required actions, and resist distraction and temptation. It is not inherited but cultivated — and if your parents did not model it, you must deliberately rebuild it. The AI forcing function (02931) intensifies this: entry-level hiring collapsed 50% since 2019 because routine tasks that once trained newcomers are exactly what AI handles. Agency is no longer optional; it is the filter between those who direct AI and those who are displaced by it.

### 2. Skill Stacking (09853, 02220, 03540, 03924)
Dan Koe's thesis, reinforced by the "How to Win When Everyone Has AI" extraction: rarity comes from the overlap of multiple skills, not dominance in a single skill (03540). Capitalism rewards things that are rare and valuable; you become rare by combining skills that rarely coexist. AI dramatically lowers the cost of combining skills — adjacent skills like coding, writing, analysis, and design become more accessible, collapsing the barrier to stacking (03540). The "Future Proof Skill Stack" (02218) identifies a combination of skills that remain valuable despite AI advancement (specific skills enumerated in the original source).

### 3. 5D Thinking (03117)
Dan Koe's 42-atom extraction describes strategic thinking as a learnable meta-skill with five dimensions. This is the cognitive architecture behind effective skill stacking — it is not enough to accumulate skills; you must think strategically about how they combine, where the gaps are, and what the market undervalues.

### 4. The Polymathic Path (09651)
The Polymathic Poet represents the existence proof: a single individual who taught himself skills conventionally considered "impossible" to self-teach. The claim is that polymathic learning is not a personality type but a methodology — the same learning science principles (top-down encoding, spaced repetition, deliberate practice) applied across domains rather than within one.

### 5. Anti-Patterns: What Not To Do (00110, 10519, 00119)
The AI engineering aspirant pieces document the failure mode: chasing credentials, following hype cycles, applying to jobs instead of building portfolios. The common thread is passivity disguised as action — doing what the market tells you to do rather than what your agency directs. "If you're still applying to jobs, read this" (00119) argues that building proof-of-work portfolios is a stronger signal than traditional applications — not that all job-seeking reflects insufficient agency, but that proof-before-application is the higher-leverage move.

### 6. Mental Models Over Information (04354, 10470)
The atom from 04354: 80 unrelated mental models are more effective for decision-making than information accumulation. "How To Think Like The Top 1%" (10470) extends this — elite thinking is not having more data but having better lenses. This connects skill stacking to the learning science cluster: the value is not in the skills themselves but in the mental models each skill installs.

### 7. Validation vs Perspective (09908)
Most people seek validation (confirmation that they are on the right path) rather than perspective (genuine insight into where they are). This is the psychological trap that prevents skill stacking: the desire for external validation locks people into established paths, while the polymathic path requires comfort with being unclassifiable.

## Synthesis
The sources construct a hierarchy: agency (meta-skill) enables skill stacking (strategy) which is powered by learning science (methodology) and evaluated by mental models (judgment). AI accelerates every layer — it amplifies agency for those who have it, lowers the barrier to skill acquisition, and makes unusual skill combinations more valuable because AI handles the common ones.

The connection to the productivity cluster is direct: the focus engineering entry (see `focus-engineering-deep-work.md`) provides the attention infrastructure for skill acquisition. The AI workflow entry (see `ai-workflow-adoption-bottlenecks.md`) documents the bottleneck shift that makes skill stacking the optimal career strategy. The learning science entry (see `learning-science-accelerated-learning.md`) provides the encoding methodology.

The deepest claim is that AI does not change the game — it reveals who was playing it. Agency was always the master skill. AI just made the consequences of having or lacking it more immediate and more visible.

## Obsolescence and Supersession

**"Single-skill mastery as the career path" was superseded by skill stacking as the rarity strategy.** The pre-AI career model was depth-first: become excellent in one domain, be compensated for that excellence. This worked when skill scarcity was domain-specific — there were not enough React developers, not enough data scientists. AI is collapsing the scarcity value of domain-specific implementation skills precisely in the areas that were previously the most legible and measurable career credentials. The correction from 03540.md and 09853.md: rarity comes from the overlap of multiple skills, not from dominance in a single skill that AI can now approximate. Capitalism rewards what is rare and valuable; uniqueness-through-combination is now more defensible than uniqueness-through-depth in AI-replicable domains.

**"Entry-level hiring" as the pipeline for developing judgment was structurally disrupted.** The traditional career pipeline assumed that junior-level tasks (boilerplate, CRUD, routine bug fixes, support tickets) were both economically valuable enough to justify hiring and educational enough to train judgment. AI handles exactly those tasks. The 02931.md extraction documents the consequence: entry-level hiring collapsed 50% since 2019. The supersession is a pipeline gap — not that junior developers disappear but that the economic justification for junior-level work that also trained judgment is gone. The question it leaves open: how is judgment now trained without the practice ground that junior-level work provided? Skill stacking may be the answer — deliberately acquiring judgment through active learning and project-building rather than passive employment progression.

**"Chasing credentials" as career strategy was exposed as passivity disguised as action.** The pre-AI career model validated credentials as signals of capability — degrees, certifications, years-of-experience — because credentials were verifiable proxies for competencies that were hard to demonstrate directly. The anti-patterns documented in 00110.md, 00119.md, and 10519.md converge: chasing credentials is doing what the market tells you to do rather than what your agency directs. The supersession is proof-of-work portfolios over credentials. This was already becoming true before AI — GitHub profiles and public projects were replacing resumes in technical hiring. AI accelerates the shift: when building is cheap, the cost of demonstrating capability directly is low, making the credential signal less valuable relative to the direct demonstration.
