## The Cournot Equilibrium in AI

The AI industry is not heading toward perfect competition with zero margins. Instead, it is converging on a Cournot equilibrium — an oligopoly where a small number of firms compete on supply rather than price (04218.md, 10930.md).

In a compute-constrained world, AI firms with differentiated models and high gross profit margins on inference do not equilibrate to commodity pricing. They predict competitors' actions and respond accordingly, maintaining non-zero profit margins. John Nash generalized this model in 1950 with the Nash equilibrium concept (04218.md).

The practical implication: the "race to zero" for inference costs that many predict will not happen as long as compute remains constrained and the number of frontier AI firms remains small.

## The Capex Regime Change

The SaaS/Web2 capital-light era is ending (03567.md). AI requires massive capital expenditure — infrastructure year for enterprises, with MCP adoption as a major trend (02086.jsonl). Enterprise AI capital expenditure projected to exceed $470 billion in 2026 (00144.md).

## Antipatterns & Lessons

- **Assuming AI margins race to zero**: Cournot dynamics protect margins in oligopolistic, compute-constrained markets (04218.md).
- **Treating stock selloffs as fundamental analysis**: Much of the SaaSpocalypse is algorithmic selling creating mispricing, not fundamental revaluation (04072.jsonl).
- **Confusing capex commitment with guaranteed returns**: Massive AI infrastructure spending creates execution risk if the revenue applications do not materialize at projected rates (03567.md).
- **Ignoring market concentration**: The gap between leading AI companies (Anthropic, OpenAI, Google) and the rest is widening, not narrowing.

## Obsolescence & Supersession

**The "race to zero" thesis (contested by Cournot dynamics)**: The dominant prior frame for AI infrastructure pricing was the cloud commodity analogy — compute follows Moore's Law, therefore inference costs race to zero, therefore foundation models become commodity infrastructure with no margin. This framing assumed that competitive dynamics in AI markets would resemble competitive dynamics in cloud storage: many providers, undifferentiated product, pure price competition. The Cournot analysis challenges this directly: in a market with a small number of differentiated oligopolists competing on supply rather than price, margins do not equilibrate to zero even as costs fall. The DeepSeek moment (January 2025) was interpreted by "race to zero" adherents as confirmation; re-examined through Cournot, it showed that even dramatic efficiency gains do not necessarily collapse margins when market concentration is maintained (04218.md).

**SaaS valuation model supersession**: Pre-2024 SaaS underwriting assumed recurring revenue, net revenue retention, and gross margin as the primary valuation axes — a model refined over a decade of SaaS IPOs. The SaaSpocalypse sell-off (2024-2025) revealed that these metrics did not capture the existential risk of software assets converting to inventory. The "SaaSpocalypse is algorithmic selling creating mispricing, not fundamental revaluation" interpretation (Cathie Wood, 03768.md) competes with the interpretation that the sell-off was a legitimate re-rating of an asset class whose moat assumptions had failed. Both cannot be right simultaneously. The resolution — which interpretation was correct — will be legible in the 2026-2028 period as enterprise AI adoption either validates or invalidates the prior SaaS growth assumptions.

**Capex regime change as paradigm break**: The SaaS/Web2 era was defined by capital-light operations — the defining insight that software could scale without proportional capex. Every valuation model from 2010-2023 was built around this axiom. The AI era inverts it: frontier model training and inference require massive capital expenditure, reversing the paradigm that gave SaaS companies their exceptional margin profiles. The firms that built capital-light intuitions into their investment theses (assuming high margins are structurally guaranteed by low capex requirements) now operate with a broken prior (03567.md).
