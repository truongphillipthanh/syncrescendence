# Platform Power Distribution: High Ground, Abundant Inputs, and Who Captures Value

> Thinking about software moats to protect against abundant code is the wrong frame. Focus on companies that will benefit most from newly abundant inputs.

## The High Ground Framework

Moats matter only if a company first owns the "High Ground" — something worth protecting (04302.md). The SaaSpocalypse indicates that point-solution software faces economic gravity, not that software as a whole is dying. A good business model alone is no longer sufficient.

**Companies without the High Ground** face existential risk from technological progress. Point-solution software is especially vulnerable when software abundance becomes a threat (04302.md).

**Companies owning the High Ground that can wield modern technology** benefit enormously — attracting top talent, capital, and distribution. Newly abundant resources have opposing effects depending on market position: High Ground owners leverage them to the detriment of weaker competitors (04302.md).

**Historical pattern** (04302.md):
- Rockefeller did not invent oil refining but controlled 90% of American refining through cost obsession and vertical integration
- Carnegie did not invent steel but produced higher quality at lower cost through chemical knowledge, then integrated backward (coke, iron ore, railroads) and forward (finished products)
- Amazon, Google, Meta, Microsoft used the early internet to break constraints in their industries, gain High Grounds, and integrate outward
- SpaceX broke cost-to-orbit by developing reusable rockets, reducing costs by an order of magnitude

**Carnegie's principle**: Prioritize knowing "how well and how cheaply you have done this thing" over "how much money you have made" — cost efficiency ensures permanency, unlike temporary profits from special market conditions (04302.md).

## The AI Application Layer Opportunity

The AI opportunity goes beyond models (10139.md). a16z's framework identifies three investment themes:

1. **Existing software categories becoming AI-native**: Traditional categories rebuilt around AI capabilities
2. **New categories where software directly replaces labor**: Categories that could not exist without AI
3. **Applications built around proprietary data and closed-loop workflows**: Defensibility through data moats and workflow ownership

Defensibility, workflow ownership, and data moats matter more than novelty as AI applications scale (10139.md).

## Skills as the New Apps

Skills stores as the new App Stores (04306.jsonl). The platform controlling which skills an agent accesses controls the distribution layer. Desktop control equals distribution control — the entity managing the agent runtime controls what capabilities the agent can exercise.

## API-First as Maximum Defensibility

API-first companies represent the most defensible position (03904.jsonl). Off-the-shelf software designed for human users is worst-common-denominator in a world where the primary software consumer is an agent.

## Industrial AI: Real-World High Ground

Detroit as "American Shenzhen" — Atomic Industries reviving AI-powered tool-and-die manufacturing (02491.jsonl). This represents High Ground capture in physical industries where the constraint is manufacturing precision, not software.

## Antipatterns & Lessons

- **Defending against abundant code instead of leveraging it**: The frame should be "who benefits from abundance?" not "how do we protect against it?" (04302.md).
- **Confusing moats with High Ground**: Moats protect the High Ground. Without the High Ground, moats protect nothing worth defending (04302.md).
- **Underwriting AI companies with SaaS metrics**: Underwriting opportunities in diverse industries is harder than SaaS because of the lack of standardized metrics and homogeneity (04302.md).
- **Hardware as automatic safe haven**: Hardware is not necessarily a strong moat even if less susceptible to AI — other technologies improve rapidly too (04302.md).
