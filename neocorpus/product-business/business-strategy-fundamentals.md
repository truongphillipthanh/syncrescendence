## Munger's Latticework of Mental Models

Charlie Munger credits a system of 80-90 mental models from multiple disciplines as the foundation behind nearly all critical decisions at Berkshire Hathaway (10997.md).

**The Hammer Problem**: To the person with only a hammer, every problem looks like a nail. The finance person sees every problem as a spreadsheet. The engineer thinks everything is a system design problem. Single-model thinking causes people to torture reality to match what they already know (10997.md).

**Models that carry the heavy lifting** (10997.md):

- **Mathematics**: Your brain does not naturally think in probabilities. Before significant decisions, estimate actual probabilities and expected values with numbers.
- **Engineering**: Backup systems (redundancy), breakpoints (thresholds where behavior changes dramatically), critical mass (self-sustaining point).
- **Biology**: Economy as ecosystem. Scale advantages: experience curve, geometry, information, social proof, distribution.
- **Psychology of misjudgment**: ~20 principles including social proof, Pavlovian association, incentive-caused bias. The "two-track analysis": (1) rational factors (real probabilities), (2) psychological factors causing wrong decisions subconsciously.
- **Microeconomics**: Competitive destruction via technology shifts. "Surfing" — catching early technology waves. Circle of competence: competing outside your competence is entering a chess tournament when your skill is plumbing.

**The Pari-Mutuel Insight**: Markets resemble racetrack betting. Most people cannot beat the system. Those who do bet rarely. Berkshire Hathaway's accumulated billions trace to approximately ten insights in the company's lifetime. The 20-slot punch card metaphor: the real returns come from a tiny number of highly calculated bets where you go all in (10997.md).

## Naval's Luck Framework (Extended)

Naval Ravikant's "How to Get Rich" framework identifies four types of luck (11005.md):

1. **Type 1 — Blind Luck**: A meteor lands in your backyard made of gold. You cannot manufacture it.
2. **Type 2 — Luck from Hustling**: Move fast, try things, increase surface area. Quantity creates conditions for quality luck. A photographer who takes 1,000 photos captures more stunning images than one who takes 10.
3. **Type 3 — Luck from Preparation**: Go so deep into a field that you spot opportunities others walk past. The antiques expert at the garage sale who recognizes the $5 lamp worth $800.
4. **Type 4 — Luck from Unique Character**: Build such a specific reputation that luck seeks you out. Warren Buffett gets offered deals nobody else sees. This takes years to decades.

**The critical insight Naval breezed past**: Types 2 and 3 are actionable immediately. Type 4 is the result of Types 2 and 3 sustained over time. The path runs through the middle two. Most people never reach Type 4 because they skip Types 2 and 3, waiting for Type 1 or trying to manufacture Type 4 without the foundation (11005.md).

**The hierarchy**: Early in journey → Type 2 (motion). Direction emerging → Type 3 (depth). Decade in with track record → Type 4 emerges as exhaust of the first two (11005.md).

## Hormozi's Growth Principles

Alex Hormozi: founder of Acquisition.com, sold Gym Launch for $46.2M, portfolio generating $250M+/year across 10 companies (02634.md, 01252.jsonl, 02770.jsonl, 02818.jsonl).

Key principles across multiple sessions (02634.md, 10066.md, 10135.md, 02772.md):
- Business growth as learnable skill, not luck
- Content as distribution strategy (books, YouTube, podcasts as marketing for portfolio companies)
- Portfolio model: invest own money, take 20-100% ownership, grow through operational involvement
- Skool.com as community platform investment
- "$100M Money Models" sold 3.6M copies in 72 hours, generating $106M — Guinness record for fastest-selling non-fiction book

## Antipatterns & Lessons

- **Spreading across dozens of projects for the feeling of progress**: Real returns come from a tiny number of highly calculated bets (10997.md).
- **Shallow preparation across many fields**: Does not build pattern recognition needed for Type 3 luck. Go deep in one domain, not broad across five (11005.md).
- **Learning models without applying them repeatedly**: Models only become useful when applied to real problems, over and over, for years. Munger admits he still makes mistakes after a lifetime of practice (10997.md).
- **Waiting for perfect moments instead of creating motion**: There is no perfect moment. There is only motion and stillness. Choose motion (11005.md).
