# CANON-3006: RESEARCH PROTOCOLS
## Methodologies and Quality Standards for Decision-Bearing Research

> Quality over quantity. Few high-confidence verdicts outweigh many unvalidated claims.

Research exists to make decisions better. Every research act serves a decision. The measure of research quality is decisional impact, never volume.

This document codifies the universal research methodology -- applicable to any agent, any domain, any decision context. It belongs in Operations, where all agents can reach it.

---

## PART I: DECISION-BEARING QUESTIONS FIRST

### The Pre-Research Gate

Before researching anything, answer four questions:

1. **What decision does this inform?** If you cannot name the decision, you are researching out of curiosity, not necessity. Curiosity is valuable, but it is not operational research.

2. **What would we do differently if X versus Y?** If the answer is "nothing changes regardless," the research is academic. Skip it.

3. **What confidence level is needed to act?** Some decisions need 90% confidence. Some need 50%. Know which before investing in the research that would provide the difference.

4. **What is the cost of being wrong?** High-cost errors demand more research investment. Low-cost errors may not justify any research at all — just decide, act, and correct.

### When to Research

Research when the answer materially changes decisions, when uncertainty blocks action, and when the time investment is justified by the decision's importance.

### When to Skip

Skip when the answer doesn't affect decisions, when uncertainty is acceptable given the decision's reversibility, or when other information is more critical to the immediate work.

The most common research failure is not insufficient rigor. It is researching the wrong question — or researching a question that doesn't need researching at all.

## PART II: THE SOURCE TRIAD METHOD

### Three Passes for Every Decision-Bearing Question

**Pass 1: Primary Sources**

Go to the origin. Academic papers. Official documentation. Company engineering blogs. Government reports. Patent filings. The people who did the work, writing about what they did.

*Evaluate*: Is this the original source or a restatement? What is the institution's credibility? How recent? What methodology was used?

*Red flags*: Secondary reporting without links to primary material. Marketing language masquerading as research. Claims presented without evidence or methodology.

**Pass 2: High-Signal Secondary Analysis**

Expert interpretation. Technical deep-dives. Research reviews. Industry analysis. Commentary from people with demonstrated domain expertise.

*Evaluate*: Does this add interpretation beyond restating the primary source? What is the analyst's demonstrated expertise? Is the analysis rigorous or speculative? Are non-obvious implications revealed?

*Red flags*: Hype without evidence. Cherry-picked data supporting a predetermined conclusion. Undisclosed conflicts of interest. Extrapolation beyond what the evidence supports.

**Pass 3: Counter-Position**

The adversarial pass. Academic rebuttals. Skeptical analyses. Limitation acknowledgments. Competing approaches. People who disagree with the emerging conclusion and can articulate why.

*Evaluate*: Are the limitations genuine? What assumptions might fail? Are there alternative explanations the first two passes missed? What are the failure modes?

*Red flags*: Strawman arguments. Dismissal without engagement. Ideological opposition masquerading as evidence-based critique. Skepticism without substance.

### Why Three Passes Are Non-Negotiable

Pass 1 alone produces naive trust. Pass 1 + Pass 2 produces informed trust. Pass 1 + Pass 2 + Pass 3 produces calibrated judgment. The counter-position pass is where most research protocols fail — it is uncomfortable to seek evidence against your emerging conclusion, which is precisely why Popper placed falsifiability at the center of scientific method. The discomfort of seeking refutation is the signal that the pass is working.

## PART III: THE VERDICTING PROCESS

### Five Components Per Research Question

For each question researched through the Source Triad, produce a verdict with five components:

**1. Claim**: A clear, testable statement — what Popper would call a conjecture bold enough to be refuted. Example: "Current frontier models maintain reliable performance over 30-hour coding sessions" -- specific, testable, actionable.

**2. Confidence Level**:
- **High (>85%)**: Multiple primary sources. Expert consensus. Replicated results. Act with confidence.
- **Medium (50-85%)**: Primary source plus analysis. Plausible but not conclusive. Act with caution.
- **Low (<50%)**: Single report. Unverified. Speculative. Act only if reversible.
- **Speculative (<25%)**: Rumor. Prediction. No evidence. Do not act unless the cost of inaction exceeds the cost of being wrong.

Every confidence level must include a rationale. "High confidence" without explanation is assertion, not assessment.

**3. Recency**:
- **Current (0-3 months)**: Likely accurate for the claimed context
- **Recent (3-12 months)**: Worth checking for updates
- **Dated (1-2 years)**: May have changed significantly
- **Stale (>2 years)**: Use with caution in any fast-moving domain

**4. Volatility**:
- **Stable**: Unlikely to change. Fundamental principles. Laws of physics. Mathematical truths.
- **Shifting**: Actively evolving. Current capabilities. Market conditions. Tool landscapes.
- **Speculative**: Not yet real. Predictions. Roadmaps. Promises.

**5. Design Delta**: "If this claim is true, then we should [specific action]."

This is the test of operational relevance. If you cannot state the design delta, the research is not decision-bearing. If someone reading the design delta cannot make a different decision from it, the delta is too vague — sharpen it.

## PART IV: CONTRADICTION MANAGEMENT

### When Sources Disagree

Sources will disagree. The protocol for contradiction:

1. **Document both claims explicitly** — do not silently discard the inconvenient one
2. **Assess relative source quality** — primary > secondary > anecdotal. Rigorous methodology > case study > opinion.
3. **Identify the resolution path** — what additional information would resolve the contradiction?
4. **Mark as "pending resolution"** — do not pretend the contradiction doesn't exist
5. **Use the most conservative estimate** for decisions while resolution is pending
6. **Flag for follow-up** if the decision is critical

### Contradiction Priority

- **Critical**: Blocks a decision currently in progress. Resolve immediately.
- **Important**: Affects strategy but not immediate action. Resolve within the current research cycle.
- **Minor**: Interesting but doesn't change decisions. Note and move on.
- **Ignorable**: No operational impact. Document only if trivial to do so.

### The Research Log

Maintain a structured log for active research:

```
Entry ID | Timestamp | Question | Source | Gist | Claims | Confidence |
Recency | Volatility | Design Delta | Contradictions | Next Actions
```

The log is an operational tool, not an archive. Prune entries that no longer bear on active decisions.

## PART V: RESEARCH ANTI-PATTERNS

### Benchmark Shopping

Selectively citing benchmarks that support the desired conclusion. Creates false confidence in decisions built on incomplete evidence.

*The pattern*: Find the benchmark where your preferred option wins. Ignore the benchmarks where it loses. Present the winning benchmark as "the evidence."

*The fix*: Include context. Cite competing benchmarks. Note what is NOT measured. A benchmark that tests coding speed but not code quality does not support claims about code quality.

### Link Dumping

Many sources without synthesis or verdict. Appears thorough but provides no guidance. The reader finishes knowing what was read but not what was concluded.

*The pattern*: "Here are 15 sources on the topic." No synthesis. No verdict. No design delta.

*The fix*: Every source must have an explicit contribution to the verdict. Few well-analyzed sources outweigh many undigested ones. If you can't state what a source contributes, remove it.

### Hype Amplification

Uncritically repeating marketing claims or speculative predictions. Vendor announcements are not evidence. Roadmaps are not capabilities.

*The pattern*: "Company X announced Y, therefore Y is real and we should plan for it."

*The fix*: Distinguish claims from evidence. Note commercial interest — the vendor has incentive to overstate. Separate current capability from roadmap promises. Current capability is evidence. Roadmap is speculation.

### Recency Bias

Assuming newer is always better. Ignoring stable principles in favor of the latest development. Creating thrash by reacting to every new announcement.

*The pattern*: Reorganizing workflows every time a new tool launches.

*The fix*: Assess volatility explicitly. Value timeless principles. Separate fundamental from fashionable. A new tool does not invalidate the principles of tool selection — it provides a new data point for the existing framework.

### Confirmation Bias

Seeking information that supports existing beliefs. The most insidious anti-pattern because it feels like research.

*The pattern*: Researching only sources that agree with the preliminary conclusion. Dismissing contradictory evidence as "low quality" without rigorous assessment.

*The fix*: Pass 3 (Counter-Position) is mandatory, not optional. Actively seek counter-arguments. Red-team your own proposals. The absence of counter-evidence in your research is more likely evidence of incomplete research than evidence of a flawless conclusion — Popper's asymmetry: a thousand confirmations cannot prove a theory, but one refutation can destroy it.

## PART VI: CONTEXT-SPECIFIC PATTERNS

### AI Capability Evaluation

Key questions: Actual versus claimed capability? Reliability under production conditions? Cost and latency trade-offs? Failure modes? Available alternatives? Adoption path?

Red flags for over-hype: Cherry-picked demonstrations. No limitation discussion. Weak baselines in comparisons. Unclear failure modes. Vendor-supplied benchmarks without independent replication.

### Paradigm Shift Assessment

Key questions: What is fundamentally changing? What is incremental improvement branded as revolution? What enables this shift now? What are the adoption barriers? What is the realistic trajectory?

Distinguish: Capability unlock (genuinely new things possible) versus efficiency gain (same things, faster/cheaper) versus interface improvement (same capability, better UX) versus hype cycle (attention without substance).

### Infrastructure Service Evaluation

Source Triad applied to infrastructure:
- Pass 1: Vendor documentation, benchmarks, pricing, SLAs, certifications
- Pass 2: Third-party benchmarks, case studies, independent technical reviews
- Pass 3: Known limitations, customer complaints, alternatives analysis, exit feasibility

Evaluation dimensions:
- **Technical**: Latency (p50/p95/p99), throughput, reliability, quality, scalability
- **Business**: Funding stability, market position, strategic risk, pricing trajectory, support quality
- **Governance**: Security certifications, privacy compliance, audit capability, incident response
- **Lock-in**: Data portability, API compatibility, feature dependency, migration cost

### Capability Contract Assessment

For any external service the operation depends on:
- Performance SLOs documented and enforced?
- Data governance (residency, retention, deletion) specified?
- Provenance (output traceability to sources) available?
- Exit strategy (migration path, transition support) tested?
- Cost accounting (transparent, predictable pricing) verified?

Decision framework: `Data Sensitivity x Latency Requirements x Economic Leverage x Lock-in Risk → OWN | LEASE | HYBRID`

## PART VII: OUTPUT FORMATS

### Research Report (comprehensive)

When: Major strategic questions. Significant uncertainty. High-impact decisions.

Structure: Executive Summary → Decision-Bearing Questions → Methodology → Findings → Synthesis → Limitations → Next Steps

Writing standard: Lead with conclusions. Clear testable claims. Separate evidence from interpretation. Acknowledge uncertainty.

### Research Log Entry (quick)

When: Targeted questions. Confirming specific claims. Updating frontier knowledge.

Structure: Question → Source → Finding → Confidence → Design Delta → Next

### Decision Brief (action-oriented)

When: Immediate decision required. Stakeholder communication.

Structure: Decision → Recommendation → Rationale → If Wrong → Timeline

## PART VIII: CAPABILITY CONTRACT AUDIT

### Audit Cadence

**Monthly**: Performance metrics versus targets. Cost versus budget. Incident scan. Degradation trend detection.

**Quarterly**: Full contract review. Vendor health assessment. Alternatives evaluation. Renegotiation if warranted.

**Annual**: Own versus lease reassessment. Full landscape analysis. Migration consideration. Long-term strategy alignment.

### Findings Classification

**Green**: All criteria met. Stable or improving. Cost as expected. No significant risks.

**Yellow**: Borderline criteria. Minor degradation. Cost creeping. Emerging risks. Action: increase monitoring, engage vendor, research alternatives.

**Red**: Critical violations. Significant issues. Cost overruns. Material risks. Action: immediate escalation, serious alternative evaluation, migration planning.

## PART IX: OPERATIONAL RESEARCH EXCELLENCE

### The Five Principles

1. **Decision-bearing questions first** — research what matters to decisions, skip what doesn't
2. **Source Triad always** — primary + secondary + counter-position for every claim
3. **Verdict with confidence** — claim + confidence + recency + volatility + design delta
4. **Intellectual honesty** — acknowledge limitations, contradictions, and uncertainty
5. **Continuous validation** — update verdicts when proven wrong

### The Standard

Actionable over academic. Every output must enable better decisions. If a research artifact does not change what someone does, it failed — regardless of how thorough it was.

The goal: make better decisions faster by knowing what is true, what is uncertain, what is changing, and what it means for action.
