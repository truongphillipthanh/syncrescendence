## 0. Executive Reading

The old shell did not have one backlog problem.
It had a backlog hydra:

- intention compass
- deferred commitments
- implementation backlog
- operational backlog
- projects CSV
- tasks CSV
- agent inboxes
- content queues
- inboxed triangulation responses

Each existed for a reason.
Together they became ungovernable.

## 1. What Was Good

### 1.1 Different backlog granularities were real

Strategic intention, operational commitment, engineering task, local office queue, and content intake are not the same thing.
The old shell was right to feel that.

### 1.2 Deferred commitments were the best execution bridge

The summit audit was clear: deferred commitments were the healthiest execution surface because they linked intentions to actual work.

### 1.3 Implementation backlog captured real engineering insight

The implementation backlog was not noise.
It was a tactical discovery surface that later failed reconciliation.

## 2. What Went Wrong

### 2.1 No one system had explicit authority

The hydra had many heads and no clear constitutional precedence.

### 2.2 Flow law was implicit

Work was supposed to move from intention to execution to done, but the actual routing law was not enforced.

### 2.3 Retirement law was weak

Old systems lingered after being functionally superseded.

## 3. Successor-Shell Consequence

The successor shell should distinguish:

- executive intention
- live implementation backlog
- office-local queues
- transient relay queues
- archival or pedigree backlog systems

But only one of those should be the live program authority.

## 4. Net Rule

Preserve the distinction between backlog types.
Do not preserve multiple competing authorities.
